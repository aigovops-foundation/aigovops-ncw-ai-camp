## 15-30 · LIVE DEMO — Deep Think + Model Council on a Real NCW Classroom Question

**This is the centerpiece of the centerpiece.** 15 minutes. One screen. One question. Multiple frontier AIs reasoning live. The room will not forget this.

### The setup (1 min on screen)

**Slide 7 — "Two ideas that change how we use AI for hard questions"**

> **DEEP THINK**: instead of giving you an instant answer, the AI spends 30 seconds to 3 minutes *thinking* — generating multiple solution paths, checking its own logic, exploring alternatives, then giving you the answer. Available in [Gemini 3 Deep Think](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/gemini-models/gemini-3-deep-think/), OpenAI's o-series, Claude's extended thinking. Costs more compute. Gets dramatically better results on hard problems.
>
> **MODEL COUNCIL**: instead of trusting one AI's answer, you ask **three frontier models the same question, in parallel**, then a fourth model synthesizes the agreement, surfaces the disagreement, and shows you the spread. Available in [Perplexity's Model Council](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/perplexity-ai_introducing-model-council-in-perplexity-activity-7425210595890769921-qRdh) (Gemini + Claude + GPT + a synthesizer), and via the open-source [AI Council Framework](https://github.com/focuslead/ai-council-framework).

> *"Why does this matter for education? Because the questions that matter most — 'is this student ready to advance?', 'is this curriculum bias-free?', 'is this AI tool safe for my 4th-grader?' — those are exactly the questions you should never trust one AI to answer alone. You ask three. You make them show their work. And then a human decides."*

### The live demo (12 min)

**The Question (chosen with educators in the room in mind):**

> *"You are advising a 4th-grade teacher in Wenatchee. She has a struggling student — limited English, two grade levels behind in reading, anxious about being called on. Design a 4-week plan that uses AI as a partner (not a replacement) to help this student catch up — and tell me how the teacher will know it's working AND that it's not causing harm. Cite real research."*

**This is the perfect test question because:**
- Multi-step (plan + measurement + harm-avoidance)
- Educator-specific (the audience is literally in the room)
- Has ethical dimensions (the harms list applies)
- No single right answer — requires synthesis

### Step 1 — Standard Gemini, fast mode (2 min)
Type the question. Get a quick, decent, kind of generic answer. Read 2-3 bullets aloud. *"This is what most people get most of the time. It's fine. Watch what happens when we ask it to think harder."*

### Step 2 — Deep Think (4 min)
Switch to **Gemini 3 Pro → Thinking Level: Deep Think**. Submit the same question. **Walk through the visible reasoning trace as it appears on screen.** This is the magic — the room watches the AI reason.

Narrate live:
> *"See? It's considering the FERPA implications. Now it's pulling apart the difference between 'comprehension' and 'fluency.' Now it's flagging that pure-AI tutoring has shown a 15% critical-thinking decline in over-reliance scenarios — that's the MIT study from yesterday — and proposing a hybrid plan. That's what 'thinking' looks like. Three minutes of compute. A teacher would have spent two hours to get here. The teacher then spends 15 minutes editing, not 2 hours from scratch."*

### Step 3 — The Model Council (6 min)
Open **Perplexity Model Council** (or run 3 separate browser tabs: Gemini Deep Think, Claude Opus extended thinking, GPT-5 reasoning). Paste the same question into all three. Then paste all three answers into a fourth tab with this synthesizer prompt:

```
You are a panel chair. Three educational AI advisors gave the answers below
to the same question about a 4th-grade ELL student.

Your job: in 5 bullets, surface:
1. Where all three agree (high-confidence recommendations)
2. Where they disagree (and what the disagreement reveals)
3. What none of them said but should have
4. What the teacher should do FIRST on Monday
5. The single biggest risk if this plan fails

Then assign a confidence score (1-5) to each of your 5 bullets.
```

Read the synthesis aloud. **The room will visibly lean forward.** This is the most underrated AI workflow in education, and almost no one in this room has seen it.

### Step 4 — The punchline (1 min)
Bob speaks:
> *"What you just watched is what the AiGovOps Foundation calls 'AI you can trust' — not because any single model is perfect, but because you didn't trust any single model. You triangulated. You made them show their work. You kept a human at the chair. That's the **two-eyes rule** with a third opinion. That's what partnership looks like."*

### Why this demo lands
- It's a **real question** about a **real student** that **real teachers** in the room actually have.
- It demonstrates **technical capability** (Deep Think) without being a tech demo.
- It demonstrates **governance** (Model Council) without lecturing about governance.
- Every attendee — teacher, business owner, nonprofit leader — recognizes: *"I can ask three AIs about my hard questions too."*

## 40-50 · The 7-Point NCW AI Partnership Pledge

This is where the room writes the future together.

### Slide 10 — "Our pledge to each other"
Project the pledge live. The room can suggest edits via QR code → Mentimeter / Google Form. Best edits get added in real time.

> **THE NCW AI PARTNERSHIP PLEDGE**
> (a living document — version 1.0, August 11, 2026)
>
> **1. AI is a partner, not a replacement.**
> We never let AI be the final word on a student's learning, a worker's job, or a community member's well-being.
>
> **2. We use AI to expand opportunity, especially for those without it.**
> Rural students. ELL students. Special-ed students. First-generation small-business owners. Native communities. AI that doesn't reach them isn't worth the electricity.
>
> **3. We govern AI like we engineer it — with checklists, evidence, and reviews.**
> Every district, every business, every nonprofit in NCW commits to a written, public AI use policy. Even if it's 5 sentences.
>
> **4. We protect critical thinking, not just outcomes.**
> If a tool makes our kids better at the test but worse at thinking, it fails. We measure both.
>
> **5. We disclose. Always.**
> When AI helped write it, we say so. We teach our kids to do the same.
>
> **6. We triangulate hard questions.**
> No single AI gets to answer a question that matters. We use Model Councils, second opinions, and Deep Think for anything important.
>
> **7. We keep the receipts.**
> Prompts saved. Outputs saved. Edits saved. So a parent, a regulator, an auditor — or our own conscience — can verify our work.

- 3 minutes for "what's missing?" — collect via QR code.

Every signer gets a badge for their website, their classroom door, their store window. **This is what an AI-positive community looks like — not a city that banned it, not a city that surrendered to it. A city that signed for it.**"*

**Pre-staged hard questions to be ready for:**

1. *"My district doesn't have the budget for Gemini Ultra. Is Deep Think only for rich schools?"* → Answer: Free Gemini and Extended Thinking are now available to all users; the Foundation's tools are Apache-2.0 free; Khanmigo has free districts program; we'll help you find the path.
2. *"Aren't you just selling AI to schools?"* → Answer: We're a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. Apache-2.0 license. No SaaS. Receipts are public.
3. *"My kid's teacher is using AI to grade. Is that legal?"* → Answer: The [AI Bill of Rights for Education](https://resources.norrageducation.org/resource/822/sneak-preview-a-blueprint-for-an-ai-bill-of-rights-for-education) says a qualified human makes final evaluative decisions. FERPA says student records get human oversight. We help districts write the policy that draws that line.
4. *"What about the deepfake of my daughter?"* → Answer: This is a school-safety issue, not an AI-policy issue. The same incident-response process you use for any abuse applies. Plus tooling for detection.
5. *"You said Stanford found +34%. How do I know that's not cherry-picked?"* → Answer: That's why we showed the harms slide too. The same evidence base also includes the MIT study showing -15%. The truth is both. Partnership means using the +34% pattern and refusing the -15% pattern.
