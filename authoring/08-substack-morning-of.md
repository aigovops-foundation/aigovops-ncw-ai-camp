# This Morning in Wenatchee, We're Choosing Which Future We Want

*By Ken Johnston and Bob Rapp, AIGovOps Foundation · August 11, 2026*

---

## Two Students. Two Futures. One Day to Choose.

It's a Tuesday morning in 8th-grade algebra. Two students in Wenatchee. Same teacher. Same week. Same town.

Maria opens her AI tutor and it already knows what she got wrong yesterday. It doesn't hand her the answer — it asks her a question, then another, then plays a 90-second video her teacher recorded for exactly this moment. Twenty-two minutes a day. Three months. By spring, she's scoring [34% higher](https://www.instagram.com/p/DZWP5QnFxuz/) than the comparison group on her state-aligned assessment. Maria wants to be an engineer at the dam on the Columbia. For the first time, she thinks she can.

Jake uses a chatbot to write his last seven English essays. His teacher suspects something but can't prove it — the detection tools are now sold by the same companies that sell the cheating tools. In second period, a deepfake image of his classmate Sara is passed around in homeroom. It was generated in four minutes by a bored 14-year-old with a free app. Sara stops coming to school. After four weeks of asking AI to tell him what's real online, Jake's own ability to spot fake news has dropped [15.3%](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jun/19/chatbots-critical-thinking-skills). He doesn't notice. He never will, unless someone helps him.

Both of these stories are real. Both happened in American schools this year.

Our job today — yours and ours, together in this room — is to make Maria's future the default, and Jake's the exception. We believe we can do that. Here's why we're here to try.

## The Evidence, Both Sides

We always show both sides. Always. It's the only honest way to have this conversation.

**The wins are real.** A [Stanford Graduate School of Education six-month randomized controlled trial](https://www.instagram.com/p/DZWP5QnFxuz/) followed 10,000 students across 200 US schools. Students paired with AI tutors scored 34% higher on assessments than the comparison group. The biggest gains came in math and science — the subjects where students struggled most. Meanwhile, [Khan Academy's own data](https://verodate.ca/blog/ai-tutors-personalized-learning-classroom-2026), shared at ASU+GSV this April, showed students using Khanmigo 30 or more minutes a week achieved 23% higher mastery rates on curriculum-aligned assessments. These are not marketing claims. They are published numbers, and they should matter to every educator, parent, and policymaker in this room.

**The harms are also real.** A [four-week MIT study reported in the Guardian on June 19th](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jun/19/chatbots-critical-thinking-skills) — yesterday, as we were finishing these slides — found that students who relied on chatbots to evaluate online information saw their own critical-thinking ability decline 15.3%. A [Cornell study of 95,000 students](https://scientificinquirer.com/2026/05/26/widespread-ai-misuse-by-college-students-signals-need-to-rethink-assessment/) found that one in three college students regularly use generative AI for assignments, with 9% admitting outright academic dishonesty. And the institutions that are supposed to set direction are genuinely divided: [29 of 51 NYC City Council members](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/09/nyregion/nyc-schools-council-members-ai-ban.html) sent a letter to the mayor asking to pause AI in schools entirely; the [American Federation of Teachers](https://www.edweek.org/technology/teachers-unions-ai-plan-calls-for-big-tech-tax-screen-bans-in-elementary-schools/2026/05) is calling for screen bans in elementary schools and a Big Tech tax; and the [US Senate — Ranking Member Blunt Rochester and Chair Tuberville](https://www.bluntrochester.senate.gov/news/press-releases/news-ranking-member-blunt-rochester-and-chair-tuberville-lead-investigation-into-ai-and-k-12-education/) — asked the GAO to investigate AI in K-12 schools, just five days ago.

Here's the honest frame: "wait and see" is not a strategy anymore. Fifty thousand schools are already using AI. The evidence says that when it's done well, kids learn 23% to 34% more. The strategy is **partner well, govern smart, and move.**

## What We'll Do Live Today

This morning at the Wenatchee Convention Center, we're running four hands-on sessions across three rooms for 300 attendees. Every session follows the same rhythm: you arrive at a 5, you leave at a 7 or 8. Show-and-tell after every round. No lectures.

**Cool Tools** is the biggest room — educators, business owners, nonprofit leaders sitting down with Gemini, Perplexity, and Gamma for the first time and leaving with something they actually built.

**Cool Schools** is for the teachers and district staff: how to use NotebookLM to build a differentiated reading set in four minutes, how to soften the parent email you've been dreading, how to write the five-sentence classroom AI policy you've been putting off.

**The Rules** is for the orchard owner, the dental front desk, the public utility — the people who need to know what not to paste into a free chatbot, what the two-eyes rule means for their business, and how to write a five-sentence AI policy for a shop with six employees.

**Build It in 60** is the small room with power strips at every table, where attendees will build a working AI agent for their actual classroom or business in under an hour, then "stress-test" each other's agents by trying to break them. That last step — figuring out what your agent will and won't do — is governance. Learned by playing.

The afternoon plenary is the centerpiece of the day. We'll run a live demonstration that almost no room in America has seen yet: **[Gemini 3 Deep Think](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/gemini-models/gemini-3-deep-think/)** — where the AI spends two or three minutes thinking through a hard question, showing its reasoning trace on screen — followed by a **[Model Council](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/perplexity-ai_introducing-model-council-in-perplexity-activity-7425210595890769921-qRdh)**, where we ask three frontier models the same real NCW classroom question in parallel, then have a fourth model synthesize where they agreed, where they disagreed, and what none of them said but should have.

The question we'll put to the Council: *You are advising a 4th-grade teacher in Wenatchee. She has a struggling student — limited English, two grade levels behind in reading, anxious about being called on. Design a four-week plan that uses AI as a partner to help this student catch up — and tell me how the teacher will know it's working AND that it's not causing harm.*

That's not a demo. That's a question real teachers in that room actually have. And watching three AIs argue toward a better answer than any of them reached alone — that's what partnership looks like.

Then we'll close the plenary the way it should be closed: by signing something together.

## The 7-Point NCW AI Partnership Pledge

This afternoon, the room will write and sign this together. Here it is in full. Version 1.0. August 11, 2026.

---

> **THE NCW AI PARTNERSHIP PLEDGE**
>
> *Signed by the citizens, educators, business owners, and community leaders of North Central Washington*
>
> We believe AI can be the most powerful partner for learning, work, and community impact this region has ever had — if we do it right. This is what we commit to.
>
> **1. AI is a partner, not a replacement.**
> We never let AI be the final word on a student's learning, a worker's job, or a community member's well-being.
>
> **2. We use AI to expand opportunity, especially for those without it.**
> Rural students. ELL students. Special-ed students. First-generation small-business owners. Native communities. AI that doesn't reach them isn't worth the electricity.
>
> **3. We govern AI like we engineer it — with checklists, evidence, and reviews.**
> Every district, every business, every nonprofit in NCW commits to a written, public AI use policy. Even if it's 5 sentences.
>
> **4. We protect critical thinking, not just outcomes.**
> If a tool makes our kids better at the test but worse at thinking, it fails. We measure both.
>
> **5. We disclose. Always.**
> When AI helped write it, we say so. We teach our kids to do the same.
>
> **6. We triangulate hard questions.**
> No single AI gets to answer a question that matters. We use Model Councils, second opinions, and Deep Think for anything important.
>
> **7. We keep the receipts.**
> Prompts saved. Outputs saved. Edits saved. So a parent, a regulator, an auditor — or our own conscience — can verify our work.

This is not a city that banned AI. It's not a city that surrendered to it. It's a city that signed for it.

## Why This Matters Beyond NCW

Wenatchee is a town of about 35,000 people. It is not a research university. It is not a coastal tech hub. It does not have the luxury of waiting for someone else to solve this.

That is exactly why it matters.

If a community here — with real orchards and real school budget pressures and real teachers who went into education to teach, not to manage chatbots — can develop a practical, human-centered AI partnership framework, then that framework works everywhere. It works in Yakima. It works in Spokane. It works in rural Georgia and rural Ohio and every other community that is watching the news from NYC and thinking: we can't afford to get this wrong, and we definitely can't afford to ban our way out of it.

The tools we're using today are the same tools available to any teacher or business owner in the country. [Gemini](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/gemini-models/gemini-3-deep-think/). [Perplexity Model Council](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/perplexity-ai_introducing-model-council-in-perplexity-activity-7425210595890769921-qRdh). Gamma. NotebookLM. The [AIGovOps Foundation's Beacon, Umbrella, and Lantern tools](https://www.aigovops-foundation.com) — which find every AI tool in your environment, translate 40+ governance frameworks into executable controls, and produce a one-page parent-facing report. All of it Apache-2.0. All of it free.

For an NCW school district, that means: Beacon scans your Google Workspace for AI tools. Umbrella tells you which COPPA and FERPA controls each one needs. Lantern answers the parent who walks into your office and asks "what AI is my kid using and what data does it have?" — in under 60 seconds.

That's not enterprise software. That's a toolkit for communities that have been told governance is too expensive or too complicated. It isn't. We built it to prove that.

The federal government is asking questions it doesn't yet know how to answer. The teachers' unions are pulling back. Two senators from opposite parties looked at the same set of facts and agreed the GAO needed to investigate. In that environment, the most valuable thing a community can do is show up, try something, document what worked, and share it openly.

That is what NCW is doing today. And when it works here, we'll put the receipts on the internet so every community like this one can start where NCW finished, not where it started.
