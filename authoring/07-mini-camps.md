## The Frame (read this once)

A **mini-camp** is a 20-30 minute self-led exercise. Each one takes a real human — a gifted student, a struggling student, a burned-out teacher, a small business owner, a community leader — and walks them through a problem they actually face, with AI as a **partner that does the heavy lifting** while the human keeps the:

- **Big decisions** (what matters, what's true, what to share)
- **Voice** (it sounds like you, not the bot)
- **Joy and creativity** (the spark stays human)
- **Final word** (you always get the last edit)

And the AI does the **painful work**:

- The blank page
- The first draft
- The boring research
- The repetitive cleanup
- The "what am I missing?" check

Every mini-camp is built on the AIGovOps Foundation's three habits:

| Habit | What it sounds like in the camp |
|---|---|
| Governance like engineering | *"Have a checklist, not a vibe."* |
| Evidence that ships | *"Save the prompt, save the answer, save your edits."* |
| Policy as code | *"Write your one rule before you start. Even one sentence counts."* |

**Five scenarios. Seven audiences. Each gets the same scenario, written in their voice, at their reading level, for their world.**

# === SCENARIO 1 — THE GIFTED STUDENT GOES DEEPER ===

**The problem:** A kid who already aces the homework is bored, and AI is being used by classmates to get the boring stuff done — but the gifted kid wants to use it to *go further*, not skip. How?

**The mini-camp:** Pick a question your class touched but didn't finish. Use AI as a research partner, a debate sparring partner, and a critic — never as the answerer. Build something nobody told you to build.

---

## 🟢 1A — For a 4TH GRADER (age 9, reading level 4)

### **My Big Project — Going Deeper Camp**

Hi! You're already good at school. That's awesome. This camp is for kids like you who finish the work and want to know **more.**

**What you'll do:** Pick something you LOVE that we only kind of talked about in class. Mine is **wolves in Washington State.**

**The 4 steps:**

1. **PICK YOUR QUESTION.** Mine: "Are wolves coming back to Washington and is that good or bad?"

2. **ASK GEMINI TO BE YOUR RESEARCH BUDDY — NOT YOUR ANSWERER.**
   Type: *"I'm a 4th grader doing a project on wolves in Washington. Ask me 5 questions to help me think harder. Don't tell me the answers."*
   See what it asks. Try to answer in your own words first.

3. **CHECK IT OUT IN REAL LIFE.** Ask Gemini for **3 places to learn more** (a book, a website, a person to talk to). Then GO and check one. Visit the library. Email the Washington Wolf Project. Watch a video. Real things, not just chatbot things.

4. **MAKE SOMETHING.** A poster. A 1-minute video. A model. **You make it. AI can help you spell-check or suggest a word, but the ideas are YOURS.**

### 🌟 The One Rule

> **AI is allowed to ask me questions. AI is NOT allowed to give me my answer. That's mine.**

### ✏️ Save Your Work

In a Google Doc called "My Wolf Project," save the **questions** Gemini asked you, and **your answers in your own words.** That's your brain growing.

## 🟢 1B — For an 8TH GRADER (age 13, reading level 8)

### **The "Go Deeper" Lab — for students who finish early**

You finish the assignment in 20 minutes when everyone else needs 50. Now you scroll your phone. That's a waste of you. Here's how to spend that extra 30 minutes building something nobody assigned.

**Pick a real question.** Not "what year did X happen" — those are 10-second answers. Pick something nobody fully agrees on. Some good ones:
- Should AI be allowed to write part of a college admissions essay?
- Was dropping the bomb on Hiroshima necessary to end WWII?
- Is the Snake River dam removal a net good for salmon, or not?

### The 5-step lab

**Step 1 — Frame it sharper.** Ask Gemini Deep Think (or the free Gemini "Extended Thinking" mode):
> *"I'm an 8th grader. I want to argue that [your position]. What are the 3 strongest arguments against me? What evidence do I need to be ready to handle them?"*

That's your real homework — answering the *other side.*

**Step 2 — Ask three AIs.** Open ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini in three tabs. Ask each: *"What's the most credible source on this question, and what does it actually say?"* Compare the three. **If they disagree, that's interesting — write down what they disagreed about. Then go read the actual source.**

This is the **Model Council** trick. You're using three AIs to triangulate truth.

**Step 3 — Read the actual source.** Yes, the actual one. A primary document, a peer-reviewed paper, a long-form article from an outlet with a real editor. AI suggests; you verify. (This is what the AIGovOps Foundation calls "truth-grounded.")

**Step 4 — Write your draft in your voice.** AI can help with: structure, grammar, what to cut. AI is NOT writing your sentences. **If you can't explain a sentence out loud to your dog, delete it.**

**Step 5 — Stress test.** Paste your draft into Gemini: *"Pretend you're a tough debate judge. What are the 3 weakest points in my argument? Don't fix them — just name them."* Then YOU fix them.

### 🌟 The One Rule

> **AI asks me questions. AI fact-checks my claims. AI argues against me. AI does NOT write my argument — that's mine.**

### 📁 The Receipts

Save your prompts AND the AI's answers in a doc called "My research log." When you turn in your project, attach it. Show your work. **That's not cheating — that's showing the world how a smart kid uses AI.**

## 🟢 1C — For a 12TH GRADER (age 17, reading level 12)

### **Building Original Work in an AI-Saturated World**

You're applying to college. Or trade school. Or going straight to work. Either way — you're about to be in environments where everyone is using AI, often badly. The skill that will set you apart is **using AI to think harder, not to think less.**

This camp is a 30-minute exercise that produces an original artifact: an opinion piece, a research memo, a project proposal, or a piece of code. The artifact has your voice on every line, AND uses AI as a partner.

### The Protocol

**1. Pick a question that doesn't have a Google-able answer.**
Bad: *"When did the EU AI Act pass?"* (one search)
Good: *"Should Washington State adopt something like the EU AI Act for K-12 schools? Why or why not?"* (a real argument)

**2. Run a Model Council.** Three frontier AIs, same prompt, in parallel:
> *"I'm a 12th grader writing a 1,200-word opinion piece arguing [position]. Give me your strongest version of the OPPOSING case, with citations to specific studies. Be ruthless."*

Open [Gemini](https://gemini.google.com), [Claude](https://claude.ai), [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com). Three tabs. Read all three opposition cases. Note what they agreed on (likely true), what they disagreed on (worth investigating).

**3. Read the primary sources they cited.** Trust nothing the AI said until you've eyeballed at least 3 actual papers or articles. ([MIT just published research](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jun/19/chatbots-critical-thinking-skills) showing that students who rely on AI to spot fake news *lose* the ability to spot it themselves — by 15.3% in 4 weeks. Don't be that student.)

**4. Outline in your voice.** Hand-write or thumb-type a one-page outline. AI is not in this step. This is where your *argument* gets built.

**5. Draft.** AI may help with: punchy intros, transitions, fact-checks on claims you make. AI does not write your thesis or your conclusion. Read every sentence out loud. If it sounds like a bot, rewrite it.

**6. Critic loop.** Three rounds, in Gemini Deep Think:
- *"Where is my logic weakest?"*
- *"What expert would disagree with me, and why?"*
- *"What did I miss?"*

Fix what's worth fixing. Reject what's not — sometimes the AI is wrong. **You're the editor.**

**7. Disclose.** Add a footer: *"I used Gemini, Claude, and ChatGPT for opposition-research, fact-checking, and structural feedback. I wrote every sentence. Prompts and responses available on request."*

That's not embarrassing. That's professional. ([It's also what the draft AI Bill of Rights for Education calls "disclosure."](https://resources.norrageducation.org/resource/822/sneak-preview-a-blueprint-for-an-ai-bill-of-rights-for-education))

### 🌟 The One Rule

> **The argument is mine. The voice is mine. The accountability is mine. The painful research, the fact-checking, the "what am I missing" stress-test — those I share with AI.**

### Why this matters for you specifically

Cornell surveyed 95,000 college students. [1 in 3 use AI regularly for assignments. 9% use it to cheat outright.](https://scientificinquirer.com/2026/05/26/widespread-ai-misuse-by-college-students-signals-need-to-rethink-assessment/) **The kids who'll get the great jobs and the great spots are the ones who can prove they're in that other 91%** — and prove it with their work.

This protocol gives you receipts.

# === SCENARIO 2 — THE CHALLENGED STUDENT KEEPS UP ===

**The problem:** A student who's behind — limited English, reading two grade levels back, anxious, maybe a learning difference. AI can do the worst thing (give them answers) or the best thing (give them another shot at understanding).

**The mini-camp:** Pick one thing you've felt stuck on for a long time. Use AI as a patient tutor who never gets tired, never sighs, and explains it as many times as you need — while you do the actual learning. Then prove to yourself you learned it.

## 🔵 2A — For a 4TH GRADER (age 9, reading level 2-3)

### **My Helper Camp — for things that feel hard**

Sometimes the words go too fast. Sometimes the page has too many words. That's OK. Smart kids feel stuck sometimes too. Here is your camp.

**Step 1: Pick the hard thing.** What is one thing in class that feels too hard? Maybe long division. Maybe reading a chapter. Maybe writing a story.

**Step 2: Ask Gemini to be your slow, kind teacher.**

Open gemini.google.com. If you can talk to it (use the mic button), even better.

Say: *"I am 9. Reading is hard for me. Please help me with [the hard thing]. Talk slow. Use small words. Use pictures or examples a 9 year old would know."*

It will try. You can say "**slower**" or "**smaller words**" or "**show me with cookies**" any time.

**Step 3: Try it yourself.** After Gemini explains, YOU try. Out loud. To your mom, your dog, your stuffed animal. **If you can teach it to your stuffed animal, you got it.**

**Step 4: Earn your sticker.** Every time you finish a hard thing, put a sticker on your folder. **YOU did the hard thing. The helper just helped.**

### 🌟 The One Rule

> **The helper is patient. The helper is kind. The helper does NOT do my work for me. I do my work. The helper helps me see how.**

### A note for your grown-up

This is good. Your kid is learning HOW to learn, not just the answer. Sit with them the first 2-3 times. After that they will fly.

## 🔵 2B — For an 8TH GRADER (age 13, struggling)

### **The "Second Chance" Lab**

If school has felt hard for a while — you're not dumb. School moves at one speed. You learn at yours. AI can be the patient tutor that never makes you feel small. Here's how to use it without it doing your homework.

### The 5-step lab

**Step 1: Name what's stuck.** One sentence. *"I don't get how to factor a quadratic. I've been faking it since October."* It's OK. Write it down.

**Step 2: Ask for the kid-sized version.** In Gemini, type:
> *"I'm in 8th grade. I'm stuck on [the thing]. Pretend I'm a smart kid who just hasn't been taught it well yet. Explain it three different ways: 1) using a real-world example a 13-year-old would care about, 2) using a drawing or picture in words, 3) using a step-by-step recipe."*

Read all three. **One of them will click.**

**Step 3: Earn it.** After it explains, ask:
> *"Now give me 3 easy problems, then 3 medium problems, then 3 hard ones. Don't give me the answers. After I try each one, I'll tell you my answer and you tell me if it's right and why or why not."*

That's not the AI doing your work. That's the AI being a tutor who has all the time in the world for you. **The doing is yours.**

**Step 4: Teach it back.** After you've done the 9 problems, open a doc and write: *"How I would explain this to a friend who's confused."* In your own words. **This is the most important step.** If you can teach it, you own it.

**Step 5: Tell your teacher.** Show them the doc. Show them the prompts. Most teachers — when you show them you're using AI to *learn harder*, not to skip — will become your biggest fan. (Some won't, and that's their problem to work on.)

### 🌟 The One Rule

> **AI never gives me the answer. AI explains it 5 different ways until ONE works. The answer? I find that. Every time.**

### 🛡️ A safety note

Don't paste your full name, your school, or anything personal into a free chatbot. ("Becca, 8th grader at WVHS, struggling with anxiety" = TOO much.) The math problem is enough.

## 🔵 2C — For a 12TH GRADER (age 17, behind on credits or skills)

### **The Catch-Up Protocol — without faking it**

You might be a few credits behind. Or you might be on track but feeling underwater. AI can be your scaffolding — without becoming a crutch that makes things worse.

### The Protocol

**Step 1: Take an honest inventory.**
What are you actually stuck on? Be specific. Not "math" — "logarithms" or "5-paragraph essays" or "how to read a research paper."

**Step 2: Make a 4-week learning plan with AI.**
> *"I'm a 17-year-old senior. I'm behind on [topic]. I have about 30 minutes a day, 5 days a week. Build me a 4-week plan to actually understand this — not memorize. Include: what to learn each day, what to practice, how to know I really got it, and how to know if I'm faking it."*

Print it. Tape it to your wall. **You executing the plan = your work. The plan itself = AI helping you organize the painful prep.**

**Step 3: Use Khanmigo or a teacher-style tutor every day.**
[Khan Academy's Khanmigo](https://www.khanmigo.com) is free for students and is specifically built **not to give you the answer.** It asks you questions. It's a real tutor. ([Stanford found students who used AI tutors like this for 22 minutes a day scored 34% higher than students who didn't.](https://www.instagram.com/p/DZWP5QnFxuz/) That's the version of AI that helps. That's this one.)

**Step 4: When you're stuck, do the "explain my confusion" move.**
> *"I'm trying to learn [X]. Here's what I think it means: [your understanding]. Where am I wrong?"*

This is more powerful than asking for the answer. AI corrects your mental model. **Your model gets better. The thing in your head gets stronger.**

**Step 5: Don't hide it.**
Tell a teacher or counselor: *"I'm using AI to catch up. Here's my plan. Here's my work. Will you check in with me weekly?"*

You'd be amazed how many adults will move mountains for a kid who shows up with a plan.

### 🌟 The One Rule

> **I never paste an assignment into AI and submit what comes out. I use AI to fix my understanding. Then I do the work.**

### Why this is the smartest move

The teacher who can't tell if you used AI? They will figure it out by April. (Detection is getting [easier, not harder.](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2026/06/20/nation/student-cheating-is-becoming-impossible-detect-an-ai-era/)) The teacher who watches you go from a D to a C to a B with your AI receipts in a Drive folder is going to write you the best recommendation letter of their life.

**Catch-up isn't cheating. Hiding is cheating. Show your work.**

# === SCENARIO 3 — THE BURNED-OUT TEACHER ===

**The problem:** It's Sunday at 4pm. Three lesson plans, eight parent emails, two IEPs, and a unit re-write to do before Monday at 7. You used to love this job. Now you're just trying to keep your head above water.

**The mini-camp:** Take back your Sunday — without losing the relationships and judgment that made you a great teacher in the first place.

---

## 🟡 3 — For a TEACHER

### **The Sunday Rescue Camp**

You didn't become a teacher to write parent emails on Sunday at 4pm. You became a teacher for the kids, the spark, the moment when a 12-year-old finally gets it. AI cannot do that part. AI cannot replace you. But AI can absolutely do the 4 hours of paper-pushing that's standing between you and the work you love.

This is a 30-minute camp to get back **2-3 hours every weekend**, starting this Sunday.

### The 6 moves

**Move 1: Lesson plan in 4 minutes (Gamma + Gemini).**

Open [Gamma](https://gamma.app). Type:
> *"5-slide lesson for [grade] [subject] on [topic]. Include 1 hands-on activity slide. Tone: warm, curious, slightly nerdy. Standards alignment: [state standard if you know it]."*

90 seconds later — a draft deck. You spend 3 minutes editing the slides for your kids, your voice, your in-jokes. **Done.**

**Why this works:** the painful part of lesson planning is the blank page. AI takes the blank page. You bring the kids.

**Move 2: Differentiate any reading in 2 minutes (Gemini).**

Paste any passage:
> *"Rewrite at 3rd, 6th, and 9th grade level. Keep all key facts. Add 3 comprehension questions per level."*

Three columns. ELL kids get the 3rd. Above-grade kids get the 9th. Everyone reads the same content. **No more 4 versions on Sunday night.**

**Move 3: IEP draft in 10 minutes (Gemini, de-identified).**

> *"I'm an SpEd teacher drafting an IEP goal in [domain] for a [grade] student. Strengths: [list]. Areas of need: [list]. Current performance: [data]. Draft 2-3 measurable annual goals with short-term objectives, aligned to the present level. Do not invent data."*

Critical: **no student names, no district names, no identifiers** in the prompt. Use "the student." You bring the data; AI brings the structure.

This is FERPA-safe. ([The FTC's amended COPPA Rule, effective April 22 2026, now includes biometric data as 'personal information' — so be even more careful about what you paste.](https://aurascape.ai/answers/ai-compliance-frameworks-standards-and-governance-for-education-institutions/))

**Move 4: The parent-email softener (Gemini).**

Type your frustrated draft. Then:
> *"Rewrite for a parent who is also tired and overwhelmed. Keep the boundary. Drop the snark. Keep my voice — warm but firm."*

Read both. Send the softened one. **Save 20 minutes of mental rehearsing.**

**Move 5: Meeting notes that take themselves ([Granola](https://granola.ai)).**

Open Granola on your laptop during your next IEP meeting, parent conference, or team meeting. It listens through your laptop (doesn't join the call as a bot). After the meeting: clean notes, action items with owners, sharable summary.

You stay present in the meeting. The notes happen. **That's the trade.**

**Move 6: NotebookLM for unit prep.**

[notebooklm.google.com](https://notebooklm.google.com). Drop in:
- Your textbook chapter PDF
- Last year's slides
- 3 articles on the topic
- The state standards doc

Then ask:
> *"Give me a 4-week unit overview, 12 lesson seeds, 5 discussion questions per week, vocabulary lists, and 3 differentiation options per lesson."*

Two hours of prep, done in 8 minutes. **You then spend the saved 2 hours doing the part only YOU can do — thinking about THIS class, with THESE kids, in THIS moment.**

### 🌟 The Teacher's Three Rules

> **1. No student-identifying info in a free tool. FERPA first, always.**
> **2. Two-eyes rule: I read every word that goes to a student, parent, admin, or in a record. AI drafts. I decide.**
> **3. The receipts: My "AI cookbook" Drive folder grows every week — best prompts, best outputs, my edits. By June I have a year of better teaching saved.**

### Write your 3-sentence classroom AI policy

> Students may use AI for **__________**.
> Students must disclose **__________**.
> I (the teacher) will use AI for **__________** and will not use it for **__________**.

Post it. Send it to parents. **You just did policy-as-code, teacher edition.**

### A gentle truth

You're not "behind" because you haven't been using AI yet. You're behind because the job has gotten impossible without it. **You are not failing. The math has changed.** This camp gives you the math back.

The work that brought you into teaching — the relationship, the spark, the moment a kid trusts you enough to risk a wrong answer in front of the class — **that's still 100% yours. AI cannot touch it. AI doesn't want to.**

## 🟡 3B — For an ADMINISTRATOR (principal, district leader)

### **The District Leadership Camp — make AI safe enough that your teachers want to use it**

You're caught between:
- Teachers who are burning out and could use the help
- Parents who saw NYT and are nervous
- A school board that wants to know your policy
- The [Senate GAO investigation](https://www.bluntrochester.senate.gov/news/press-releases/news-ranking-member-blunt-rochester-and-chair-tuberville-lead-investigation-into-ai-and-k-12-education/) that just landed in district inboxes
- The [AFT calling for screen bans](https://www.edweek.org/technology/teachers-unions-ai-plan-calls-for-big-tech-tax-screen-bans-in-elementary-schools/2026/05)
- An IT budget you don't have

Here's the 30-minute version of the move you need to make.

### The 5 moves

**Move 1: Inventory what's already in your district.**

Right now — without buying anything — open a Google Sheet titled "District AI Inventory." Columns: Tool name · Who's using it · For what · Data type touched · Approved? (Y/N/?) · Last reviewed.

Ask every principal to fill in the rows for their building. **You will be shocked.** Most districts find 15-40 AI tools already in use — most never approved, most touching student data.

This is what the AIGovOps Foundation's [**Beacon** tool](https://www.aigovops-foundation.com) does for enterprises automatically — and Beacon is Apache-2.0 free if your IT wants to use it. But the spreadsheet works.

**Move 2: The traffic-light policy.**

You don't need a 200-page document. You need three columns:
- **🔴 RED — Never:** Grading students. Deciding placement or discipline. Anything touching IEPs without human authorship. Anything touching FERPA records in a free tool.
- **🟡 YELLOW — With care:** Student research and exploration (with disclosure). Teacher drafting (with human review). Parent communication drafts.
- **🟢 GREEN — Encouraged:** Lesson planning. Differentiated reading. Translation. Accessibility (alt text, captions, voice-over). Meeting summaries.

This is roughly what [NYC adopted in March 2026](https://phys.org/news/2026-06-york-city-ai-schools-fury.html) — and what 29 of 51 NYC Council members called "too weak." The fact is: districts that pause entirely will lose teachers to districts that don't. Districts that go fully open will lose parents. The traffic light is the defensible middle.

**Move 3: Build the parent letter.**

Use Deep Think to draft. Send it home. Three paragraphs:
1. What AI we use, why, and how (in 3 sentences)
2. What we will NEVER use AI for (the red list)
3. How you (the parent) can opt out, ask questions, or escalate

Sign it personally. **The act of writing it builds the policy.**

**Move 4: Train the teachers.**

Free options:
- aiEDU classroom curriculum → [aiedu.org](https://aiedu.org)
- NCESD AI resources
- Common Sense Education AI literacy
- NCW Tech Alliance AI in Action follow-up series → [ncwtech.org/ai-in-action](https://www.ncwtech.org/ai-in-action)
- [Khan Academy's free Khanmigo Districts program](https://www.khanmigo.com)
- AIGovOps Foundation's open-source training packs → [aigovops-foundation.com](https://www.aigovops-foundation.com)

You don't need a contract. You need to designate **one AI lead per building** and give them 2 hours a month + a small stipend. That's it.

**Move 5: The board update.**

Quarterly. Three slides:
1. **What's in use.** (The inventory.)
2. **What changed.** (New tools added, retired, escalations.)
3. **Outcomes.** (Test data, teacher hours saved, incidents handled.)

Show your work. The board will calm down. The parents will calm down. The teachers will trust you. **That's governance that runs like engineering, at the K-12 scale.**

### 🌟 The Administrator's Three Rules

> **1. No tool touches a kid's record without a written policy and a human in the loop.**
> **2. We inventory quarterly. We retire what isn't being used. We escalate what causes harm.**
> **3. We trust our teachers to be professionals. We give them tools, training, and air cover.**

### The hardest truth for administrators

Banning won't work. Free-for-all won't work. **Governance is your only move.** It's also your competitive advantage. The districts in this region that get this right will recruit teachers from the ones that don't.

# === SCENARIO 4 — THE SMALL BUSINESS OWNER ===

**The problem:** You opened an orchard / a bakery / a dental office / a real-estate practice / a print shop. You did NOT sign up to be a copywriter, an HR director, a social media manager, a meeting note-taker, and a spreadsheet-cleaner — but here you are at 9pm on a Tuesday, doing all five.

**The mini-camp:** Get 5 hours of your week back this month. Without leaking customer data. Without sounding like a bot. With your hand on every important decision.

---

## 🟠 4 — For a SMALL BUSINESS OWNER

### **The 5-Hours-Back Camp**

You have to be the face of your business. Customers want to talk to **you**, not a chatbot. But you don't have to write every email, draft every social post, summarize every meeting, or clean every spreadsheet yourself. Here's the deal:

**AI does the painful invisible work.** You do everything customer-facing.

### The 5 moves, in priority order

**Move 1: Email drafting (1-2 hrs/week saved).**

Open Gemini (or Gemini-in-Gmail if you have a Google Workspace account). Train it once on your voice:
> *"I run [a bakery / an orchard / a dental practice] in Wenatchee. My voice is warm, plain, a little dry-funny, never corporate. From now on, when I ask you to draft an email, sound like me — not like a customer-service bot. Got it?"*

Then for each email:
> *"Draft a 4-sentence reply to this customer who [complained / asked / inquired]. Acknowledge their concern, explain what we'll do, close warmly. Keep my voice."*

Read it. Edit it. **Send it.** No customer ever knows AI drafted it because YOU sent your final version.

**Move 2: A week of social posts in 5 minutes (Gemini + Canva).**

Once a week, on Friday at 4pm (your worst time for creative work), open Gemini:
> *"I run [business]. Draft 5 short social posts for next week. Topics: [a seasonal hook] + [a customer story I can tell with permission] + [a behind-the-scenes moment] + [a product highlight] + [a community shoutout]. Warm voice, no hashtags, max 50 words each."*

Paste each into Canva. Add a photo (you took, or AI-generated). Schedule. **5 minutes for the week.**

**Move 3: Meeting notes that write themselves (Granola).**

[granola.ai](https://granola.ai) — $18/mo after a trial. Listens to your Zoom, Teams, Meet meetings through your laptop. Doesn't join as a bot. After the meeting: clean summary + action items with owners.

Stop taking notes. **Stay present. Look people in the eye.** That's the part of business AI can't do.

**Move 4: The spreadsheet whisperer (Gemini in Sheets).**

Open any spreadsheet in Google Sheets. Click the Gemini icon. Try:
- *"Flag any row where the price is more than 2 standard deviations from the average."*
- *"Create a summary chart of sales by month for the last 12 months."*
- *"Identify the 10 customers who haven't purchased in 90+ days."*

Boring data work that took you an hour: done in 30 seconds.

**Move 5: Rehearse the hard conversation.**

The firing. The refund. The vendor dispute. The donor ask. Type:
> *"Role-play as [the angry customer / the vendor / the donor]. I need to [tell them X]. Be as tough as the real conversation. I'll respond, you respond back, until I get good at it."*

10 minutes of rehearsal beats 20 minutes of after-the-fact regret. **The conversation is still yours. You're just better prepared.**

### 🌟 The Small Business Owner's Three Rules

> **1. NEVER paste private data into a free tool.** No customer credit cards. No patient info. No employee SSNs. No contracts marked confidential. *(If you wouldn't email it to a stranger, don't paste it into ChatGPT.)*
> **2. Two-eyes rule.** Anything that goes to a customer, regulator, the IRS, or the bank — YOU read it first. Even when AI is right 99 times.
> **3. Keep the receipts.** Make a "AI receipts" folder in Drive. Prompt + answer for anything important. You'll thank yourself the first time something goes sideways.

### Write your 5-sentence business AI policy

> At **___________** (business), we use AI to **___________**.
> We never use AI for **___________**.
> The person responsible is **___________**.
> We review our AI use every **___________**.
> If something goes wrong, we **___________**.

Print it. Post it in the break room. Done. **You just shipped a policy.**

### When you outgrow the free tier

For ~$20-30/user/month, [Glean](https://glean.com) searches across all your business files (Drive, Slack, email, CRM) and answers questions with permissions respected. *"What did we tell the Wenatchee Co-op last March?"* → the actual email thread.

When you grow past Glean, the [AIGovOps Foundation's Beacon tool](https://www.aigovops-foundation.com) (Apache-2.0, free) inventories every AI in your business and checks it against 23 audit frameworks. **For when your bank, your insurer, or your biggest customer asks "what's your AI policy?"**

### The truth about small business and AI

You opened the business because you loved doing the work — or being with the people, or making the thing. **Every hour AI saves you is an hour you get back for the part you loved.**

That's not "AI replacing you." That's you, **rehiring yourself** for the job you actually wanted.

# === SCENARIO 5 — THE COMMUNITY LEADER ===

**The problem:** You see a real community problem — affordable housing, addiction, broadband gaps, a school crisis, a small-business closure wave, climate impacts on the orchards. You're not paid to solve it; you're the one who notices. You have a tiny team or no team. AI could 10x you — or it could turn you into a propagandist of slop nobody trusts.

**The mini-camp:** Move a real community problem one step closer to action, using AI as a research partner, drafter, and visualizer — while keeping every decision, every relationship, and every voice line firmly yours.

## 🟣 5A — For a COMMUNITY LEADER (nonprofit ED, civic volunteer, board chair)

### **The Civic Action Camp — 30 minutes from concern to first move**

You care about the thing nobody else has time to care about. You've talked about it for two years. Today you move it.

### The 4 moves

**Move 1: Sharpen the problem (Gemini Deep Think).**

Don't start with the solution. Start with the question. Type:
> *"I'm a community leader in [town]. The problem I see is [your concern]. Help me sharpen this into 3 specific, actionable questions a small civic group could realistically answer in 90 days. For each question, suggest: who would have the data, who would be the natural ally, and what's the smallest first step."*

Read it. Pick the question that makes your gut tighten. That's the right one.

**Move 2: Triangulate the truth (Model Council).**

Ask three AIs the same question — same Model Council move from the centerpiece plenary:
> *"What does the credible evidence actually say about [the problem] in similar communities? What's been tried, what worked, what failed, and what's the most surprising finding nobody talks about?"*

Open Gemini, Claude, ChatGPT. Three tabs. Read all three. Note where they agree, where they disagree, what each one cited.

Then — and this is the most important step — **read the actual cited sources.** AI is a starting point, not the destination. Truth-grounded means a human verified.

**Move 3: Draft your "1-page case for action."**

Type:
> *"Draft a 1-page case for action to bring to my city council / school board / nonprofit board. Topic: [problem]. Audience: [busy elected officials / board members]. Tone: clear, urgent but not alarmist, factual, ends with 1 specific ask. Include: 3 facts with citations, 1 local story, the cost of doing nothing, and the cost of the ask."*

Edit it in YOUR voice. Add the local story (you know the story; AI doesn't). Cut anything that sounds like a press release.

**Move 4: Build the website / one-pager in 60 minutes ([Lovable](https://lovable.dev) or [Framer](https://framer.com)).**

You don't need a designer. Paste this into Lovable:
> *"Build a single-page site for a small civic campaign about [problem in town]. Include: hero with the ask, 3 facts with sources, a local story section, who's behind this (just my name), and a sign-up form. Brand: warm and trustworthy, not slick. Color: [your community's colors]. Mobile-first."*

60 seconds later: a real site. Edit by prompting. **Share the link in your next email to allies.**

This is what the AIGovOps Foundation calls "evidence that ships." You moved from concern → action in 30 minutes.

### 🌟 The Community Leader's Three Rules

> **1. No real names, no medical info, no anyone-else's-data in a free AI tool without permission.** Stories from your community get told by you, not by a bot, and with consent.
> **2. Verify every fact AI gives you against an actual source.** A community campaign built on a hallucinated stat is a community campaign that dies the day the local reporter Googles it.
> **3. Disclose.** *"This research memo was drafted with AI assistance and human verification."* Put it in the footer. The right people will trust you MORE for saying so.

### The hardest truth for community leaders

AI doesn't make a community. **You make a community.** AI makes you 5x faster at the boring stuff so you have time for the part nobody else can do — being in the room, listening, building trust over years.

The neighborhoods that win this decade are the ones with leaders who use AI to multiply their capacity for the human work — not replace it.

## 🟣 5B — For a SMALL-CITY OR LOCAL GOVERNMENT LEADER (mayor, city council, county commissioner)

### **The Local Government Camp — practical, accountable, public**

You serve a small city or county. Your constituents are watching the news — the [Senate GAO investigation](https://www.bluntrochester.senate.gov/news/press-releases/news-ranking-member-blunt-rochester-and-chair-tuberville-lead-investigation-into-ai-and-k-12-education/), the [NYC Council pause letter](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/09/nyregion/nyc-schools-council-members-ai-ban.html), the AFT calls for screen bans — and asking you what your city's position is. You don't have a $5M budget for an AI advisor. Here's the 30-minute version.

### The 5 moves

**Move 1: Inventory.** Same spreadsheet move as the school administrator above. Every department, every tool. (City clerk, planning, parks, police, library, schools.) Most small cities find 8-20 AI tools already in use — most never approved.

The AIGovOps Foundation's free [Beacon tool](https://www.aigovops-foundation.com) automates this if your IT lead wants it. Either way, **you can't govern what you can't see.**

**Move 2: Adopt a 1-page AI use ordinance.**

Three sections:
1. **What we use AI for** (drafting, summarizing public comments, translation for non-English-speaking residents, accessibility)
2. **What we will NEVER use AI for** (decisions that affect a resident's rights, benefits, or access to public services without human review; surveillance without a warrant; final hiring decisions; any decision about a child without two-eyes review)
3. **Public accountability** (quarterly disclosure of which AI tools we use, a public-comment process before adopting new ones, and a named city employee who is accountable)

This is what the AIGovOps Foundation calls **policy-as-code at the municipal scale.** It fits on one page. It's defensible. It's modular.

Draft template available at [aigovops-foundation.com](https://www.aigovops-foundation.com).

**Move 3: The community engagement move.**

Use [Gemini Deep Think](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/gemini-models/gemini-3-deep-think/) to draft a 4-question community survey:
> *"What AI uses in our city services would help you most? Where are you most worried? What do you want us to NEVER let AI do? Who do you want to be accountable?"*

Distribute via your existing channels. (Don't AI-generate the responses — that's the opposite of governance.) Synthesize the responses using [NotebookLM](https://notebooklm.google.com) — drop in all the responses, ask for themes, surface the 5 things residents care about most.

Then go to a city council meeting and **read those 5 things out loud.** That's how trust gets built. AI helps you process. Residents drive the agenda.

**Move 4: Translate everything you publish.**

NCW has significant Spanish-speaking and indigenous communities. Use Gemini to translate every public notice, every council agenda, every key document into Spanish. ([The draft AI Bill of Rights for Education](https://resources.norrageducation.org/resource/822/sneak-preview-a-blueprint-for-an-ai-bill-of-rights-for-education) names access for non-English speakers as a core right; this principle applies to civic life too.)

This is the most important equity move AI enables for a small city. **It costs essentially nothing. It changes who gets to participate.**

**Move 5: The annual public AI report.**

Once a year, publish:
- Every AI tool in use across city departments
- Any incidents, escalations, or complaints
- The savings (staff time, $) by department
- What's working, what's not, what's changing

Make it short. Make it readable. **Make it boring** — boring annual reports are what trust looks like in local government.

The AIGovOps Foundation's [Lantern tool](https://www.aigovops-foundation.com) (in early development, free, open-source) is built exactly for this: turning policy-as-code into human-readable reports an auditor — or a resident — can understand.

### 🌟 The Local Leader's Three Rules

> **1. No AI decision without human review touches a resident's rights or benefits.**
> **2. Every AI use is disclosed publicly. Every quarter. By name.**
> **3. We govern with our residents, not above them. AI helps us listen better, not louder.**

Big cities will spend years and millions arguing about AI. **You can lead by example in 30 days.** A 35,000-person city with a one-page AI ordinance, a public inventory, and an annual report is more advanced than most Fortune 500s.
