The evidence on the table

Both things are true

AI in education is producing real, measurable gains — and real, measurable harms. We're not here to sell or to scare. We're here to read the studies and decide what we do about them.

📈The wins

  • +23%

    Mastery gain on curriculum-aligned assessments for students using Khanmigo 30+ min/week (Khan Academy data shared at ASU+GSV April 2026). Khan Academy's own June 2026 A/B results add +6.1% in next-item correctness across ~20 product tests. Khan Academy / AIMS →

  • +23%

    Improvement for students using Khanmigo, the tutor built to ask questions rather than hand over answers. Khanmigo / Verodate →

  • +6.1%

    Measured math gain in the Khan AIMS study — modest, durable, and biggest for the learners who struggle most. AIMS Collaboratory →

  • Equity-positive overall

    A systematic review in npj Science of Learning finds consistent gains — strongest for ELL, special-ed, and rural students. npj Science of Learning review →

⚠️The harms

  • −15.3%

    Drop in students' own ability to spot fake news after four weeks of over-relying on AI to do it for them (MIT). MIT / The Guardian →

  • 1 in 3

    College students regularly use GenAI on assignments; 9% admit outright cheating (Cornell, 95,000 students). Cornell, May 2026 →

  • Deepfakes & cyberbullying

    AI-generated deepfakes are a fast-rising K-12 cyberbullying threat — exactly what happened to Jake's classmate Sara. EdTech Magazine →

  • The policy backlash

    29 of 51 NYC Council members urged a school AI pause; the AFT called for screen bans; a Senate GAO probe is underway. NYT → · EdWeek → · Senate GAO →

Banning won't work. A free-for-all won't work. The only move left is governance — run like engineering, sized for a small town.

Synthesis · human decides

All three agree on the spine: AI handles the painful prep (leveled texts, vocab cards, translated parent notes) while the teacher owns every interaction with the child and keeps prompts in a Drive folder. Where they differ — how much oral practice to automate — is exactly the call a human teacher should make. That's the whole point: AI converges the easy parts so your judgment goes where it counts.

    See the full question bank
    "Aren't you just replacing teachers?"

    No. Commitment #1 is that AI is a partner, not a replacement. The teacher owns every interaction with a child. AI does the four hours of paperwork so the teacher gets back to teaching.

    "What about the cheating and the deepfakes?"

    Real and rising — that's Jake's story. The answer isn't a ban (bans push it underground); it's disclosure, new assessments, and keeping the receipts so misuse is visible and teachable.

    "We're a small district with no budget. Where do we start?"

    A spreadsheet inventory, a one-page traffic-light policy, and a parent letter. All free, all today. The Foundation's open-source tools automate the rest at no cost.
